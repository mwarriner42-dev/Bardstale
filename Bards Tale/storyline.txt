Page One
He stepped away from the truck to give himself a moment and relieve himself.

The engine idled behind him, a low, steady rumble that vibrated through the soles of his boots. He kept one hand on the door as he relieved himself, eyes on the horizon out of habit more than fear. Old habits were hard to kill. Some of them were the only things that had survived.

The sun was sinking low, spilling gold across the valley. Light caught on broken stone and twisted metal, softening their edges, turning ruin into something almost gentle. Wind moved through the tall, dry grass in slow waves, and for a heartbeat it looked like water again. Like the world remembered what it had once been.

He hadn’t meant to stop and look. That was the dangerous part. Looking.

His gaze drifted lower, following the slope of the land down into the valley below.

At first, he thought the ground itself was moving.

Then he saw the legs.

A dung beetle — vast beyond reason — pushed its burden through the valley with slow, unstoppable patience. Its shell was a patchwork of chitin and metal, plates fused and bolted together over time. Pistons and cables moved with the rhythm of muscle, not replacing it, but reinforcing it.

The ball it rolled was enormous. A compacted mass of scrap and wreckage, crushed vehicles and twisted beams pressed together into a sphere the size of a mall. It groaned softly as it turned, metal grinding against metal, the sound carrying up the valley like distant thunder.

The beetle leaned into it, every step deliberate. Ancient instinct, scaled up to meet a world that had left too much behind.

The sunset painted the creature and its burden in copper and fire. For a moment, it was beautiful. Not in spite of what it was, but because of it.

Life, doing what it had always done.

A sharp cry cut through the air behind him.

Page Two
He turned.

The truck door stood wide open.

For a split second, his mind refused to accept it. He looked inside — empty seats, loose straps swaying gently, the engine still idling like nothing was wrong.

“Dad?” he called, already moving.

He rounded the front of the truck and saw him across the road, wandering into a field of tall cactus, half‑dressed and barefoot, the cat darting frantically between his legs. The animal cried again, sharp and insistent, throwing itself into his path, trying to stop him.

“Hey! Hey—!” The words tore out of him as he ran.

The cactus tore back.

Spines bit into his calves and thighs as he pushed through, swearing, stumbling, pain flaring bright and immediate. The cat weaved past him, circling the old man, brushing against his legs, refusing to leave his side.

“Dad, stop!” he shouted, reaching for him.

Above them, metal clanged softly.

The girl sat astride the roof of the truck, legs hooked around the rack as she adjusted the satellite dish. A tablet rested against her knee, lines of data scrolling as she fine‑tuned the connection. She tapped once, then again — and smiled as the signal locked in.

She glanced back toward the shouting, watching the man chase his father through the cactus field.

Then she looked down the road.

It stretched straight and empty into the distance, heat shimmering above the cracked asphalt. Something far off caught the light — a brief glint of metal. A thin smear of smoke rose beyond it, dark against the sky.

Her smile faded.

Page Three 
He caught up to his father just as the old man reached out again.

“Dad—don’t,” he said, grabbing his wrist.

The cactus loomed over them, thick and ribbed, its skin a dull green broken by scars where spines had snapped and regrown. Pale flowers bloomed along its crown — small, waxy things with bruised‑purple centers. Rare. Valuable. Dangerous.

The old man smiled at them like they were treasures.

“See?” he said softly, fingers trembling as they brushed the air just short of the spines. “They’re still here.”

The flowers only bloomed for a few days each year, fed by heat and neglect. People used to risk a lot for them — ground them down, brewed them into visions strong enough to drown memory or sharpen it to a blade. Some said they let you see the world the way it used to be. Others said they showed you what was coming.

Most never came back the same.

The cat pressed itself against the old man’s leg, tail flicking, eyes fixed on his hands.

“Dad,” he said again, firmer now. “We can’t.”

The old man frowned, confused, then winced as a spine finally found skin. He hissed in pain, pulling back, the moment breaking like glass.

Above them, something chimed softly.

Page Four
The chime came again, sharper this time.

The girl’s fingers moved quickly across the tablet as she swung the long‑range camera toward the road. The image snapped into focus — heat distortion peeling away to reveal movement beneath it.

Vehicles.

Fast.

Too fast.

She zoomed in, jaw tightening as the shapes resolved — armored frames, mismatched plating, weapons mounted where headlights used to be. Dust and smoke trailed behind them like a wake.

Bandits.

She looked down at the others, already opening her mouth to shout.

The device around her neck vibrated instead.

A voice slipped into the space between them — calm, familiar, and completely out of place.

“I believe this is the part where someone says, ‘They’re coming in hot.’”

The girl froze.

The voice continued, almost cheerful.

“Multiple hostile vehicles. High speed. Aggressive posture.”
“Estimated time to contact: under one minute.”

Below her, the cat bristled.

The road no longer felt empty.

Page Five
He didn’t argue with his father this time.

He just scooped him up.

The old man laughed in surprise, arms flailing for a second before settling, light as he’d ever been. The cat leapt clear, then immediately returned, pacing beneath them as he carried his father back toward the truck.

“I’ve got you,” he muttered, more to himself than anyone else.

Metal clanged as the girl slid down the ladder into her station, boots hitting the floor hard. She shoved aside empty bottles and crushed wrappers, clearing space with one sweeping motion.

“Hey,” the voice chimed mildly. “You know, if you cleaned once in a while—”

She groaned. “Later.”

Her fingers danced across the controls. The road appeared on one screen, the valley on another. A third bloomed into a schematic — their position, the approaching vehicles, red markers closing fast.
  
“Defensive systems online,” the voice announced.
“Window plating deploying.”
“Targeting arrays active.”

Outside, armored plates slid down over the truck’s windows with a heavy, final sound. Hidden mounts shifted beneath the hull, weapons tracking silently toward the road.

He buckled his father into the seat, hands shaking just a little. The cat jumped up, settling into the old man’s lap like it had always belonged there.

He slipped the headset over his father’s ears.

“Start Take Five,” he said.

A soft drum beat filled the cab — barely there at first, gentle and familiar. The old man’s shoulders relaxed, his eyes drifting, the world narrowing to rhythm and memory.

“Procedure initiated,” the voice replied. “Good choice.”

Outside, engines roared closer.

The music grew louder.

Page 6
The shutters slid fully into place.

Slatted armor sealed the cab windows, the outside world breaking into narrow bands of motion and shadow. The sound changed — deeper now, more contained.

“Cab shutters closed.”

The truck took a hit of uneven ground and didn’t flinch. The steering fought him for a moment, then settled. He adjusted without thinking, hands steady, jaw tight.

The scout car broke formation.

It surged ahead of the others, low and fast, cutting wide to test their speed. The motorcycles followed, splitting into pairs, engines screaming as they fanned out.

The girl’s fingers danced.

“Tracking multiple vectors.”
“Remote station active.”
“Tracking only.”

Something unfolded above them — not dramatic, not loud. Just a shift in weight, a quiet mechanical readiness.

The heavy truck in the rear convoy slowed.

Too slow.

“Heavy weapons platform aligning.”

The warning came a heartbeat before the flash.

The shot cracked past them, tearing through dust and air, close enough to feel. The truck shuddered as debris rattled against the hull.

The girl didn’t blink.

Her screen flared.

“Countermeasure deployed.”

The world outside warped — dust kicked up in a sudden wall, the shot’s follow‑through thrown wide. The second round went nowhere near them.

A pause.

“Effectiveness…”
“…inconclusive.”

The scout car hesitated.

Just for a second.

It was enough.

Page 7
The truck held.

The engine roared, heat climbing, fuel ticking down faster now. He felt it through the wheel, through the seat, through his bones. The truck wasn’t fast — but it was relentless.

The bandits adjusted.

The motorcycles pulled back, regrouping. The scout car dropped to the side, no longer probing. The heavy truck slowed again, repositioning.

They weren’t panicking.

They were thinking.

The girl finished her drink and crushed the empty pouch in her fist. She slid it back into the drawer without looking.

The device at her throat pulsed once more.

“They’re reassessing.”

The music swelled slightly in the cab, the saxophone threading through the noise. The old man’s head lolled gently with the motion, eyes closed, breathing slow. The cat stayed curled in his lap, unbothered.

The road stretched ahead.

The truck kept running.

And behind them, the bandits decided this wasn’t going to be easy.

Page 8
The road ended in fire.

Burned‑out cars lay piled across the asphalt, twisted frames fused together, blackened shells stacked nose to nose like a barricade built from old mistakes. The truck bore down on it fast, suspension tightening as the headlights washed over scorched metal and shattered glass.

He saw it instantly.

No time to brake. No room to turn.

“Off road,” he said.

The wheel jerked hard as he committed, the truck leaving the pavement in a roar of dust and stone. The ground dropped unevenly beneath them, the suspension screaming as the weight shifted, tires chewing into dirt and scrub.

Behind them, the motorcycles surged.

Two broke formation, engines screaming as they closed the gap, riders leaning low, passengers clinging tight. They came in fast and reckless, skimming the truck’s flanks, close enough that he could feel the vibration through the cab.

The girl’s screen flared.

“Close‑range threat.”

Something pulsed beneath the hull.

One of the passengers vanished.

Not exploded. Not torn apart. Just gone — thrown sideways in a violent blur as the bike swerved hard, rider fighting to stay upright before peeling away, dragging chaos back toward the rest of the pack.

The second bike didn’t slow.

The passenger leapt.

Boots hit metal with a hollow clang as the bandit slammed onto the truck’s side, fingers finding purchase, body hauling upward with feral determination. The truck bucked over broken ground, but the bandit held on, climbing fast.

The scout car broke right, cutting toward the dirt road, angling to box them in.

“Priority target,” the voice said.

The remote station rotated.

Once.

The shot was clean.

The scout car shattered in a bloom of dust and flame, flipping end over end before disappearing into the scrub. The sound hit a second later, a deep, final crack that echoed across the open ground.

The bandit reached the roof.

Metal rang as fists and boots slammed down onto the turret housing. The blows were wild, furious, relentless. Something snapped. Something jammed.

“Remote station offline.”

The truck lurched again.

Above them, the bandit found the trapdoor.

It wasn’t locked.

The latch gave way with a sharp metallic click, and the hatch flew open, sunlight pouring into the cab in a blinding shaft. A face appeared in the opening — eyes wide, teeth bared, skin streaked with dust and sweat.

Deranged. Exultant.

The girl didn’t scream.

The AI did.

A sharp, panicked sound tore through the cab, raw and wrong.

“Drive,” he said. “Both of you.”

He unbuckled in one smooth motion, rising from the seat as the truck thundered over uneven ground. His hand disappeared beneath his jacket and came back with a heavy pistol, black and familiar, drawn from a holster no one had ever seen.

The bandit dropped.

Boots hit the floor hard as the figure lunged forward, a snarl tearing free as it charged.

He raised the pistol.

A shot rang out.

Page 9
The bandit didn’t stop moving until the girl forced him to.

She crossed the cab in two steps, shoved the protagonist back into the seat, and slammed the belt home with a practiced yank. Blood soaked through his jacket, dark and spreading, but she didn’t hesitate. She tore fabric, pressed hard, wrapped tight.

“Stay,” she said. Not to him. To the moment.

The AI spoke over her shoulder, voice strained and sharp.

“Multiple hostiles closing. Boarding imminent.”

“Brake on my mark,” she said. “Hard.”

Her hand closed around the pistol he’d dropped. It felt heavier than she expected.

The truck lurched as she climbed back to her station, fingers flying. The road narrowed ahead, dirt packed thin and uneven, scrub clawing at the sides. Behind them, the bandit truck surged closer, figures clinging to its frame, shouting, reaching.

“Distance closing,” the AI warned.

“Now.”

The brakes slammed.

The truck shuddered violently, weight shifting forward as the pursuing vehicle plowed into the one behind it. Metal screamed. Bodies flew. The impact tore the formation apart in a chaos of dust and wreckage.

One bandit made it to the roof anyway.

The trapdoor burst open again, light flooding the cab as the figure dropped through.

The girl didn’t scream.

She raised the pistol and fired.

The body fell back out of sight, the hatch slamming shut behind it.

For a heartbeat, everything went quiet.

Then the cameras caught movement.

Not vehicles.

Too fast.

The AI hesitated — just long enough to matter.

“Unidentified biological mass,” it said. “Multiple.”

The dust parted.

Something low and fast streaked across the ground, fur matted, teeth flashing. A bandit hanging from the wrecked truck vanished in a blur, dragged screaming into the scrub. Another shape slammed into the side of the vehicle, claws scraping metal before peeling away, uninterested.

The remaining truck stopped firing.

Figures scattered.

The mice didn’t chase the truck.

They didn’t care.

They harvested what had fallen.

Inside the cab, the girl locked the hatch and pushed the throttle forward. The truck surged down the narrow road, suspension screaming, engine burning hot.

The cameras watched as the dust settled behind them.

Watched the movement fade.

Watched the wasteland reclaim what lingered.

The road stretched ahead.

The truck kept going.

Page 10
The truck didn’t slow until the road widened and the ground firmed beneath the tires.

By then, the engine was running hot, the suspension whining with every correction. The girl eased off the throttle only when the AI told her the cameras showed nothing but dust and distance behind them.

“No pursuit detected.”

She didn’t answer. She just kept driving.

The protagonist sagged in his seat, breath shallow, eyes unfocused. The makeshift bandage was soaked through, her hands slick with blood she refused to look at for too long.

“Stay with me,” she said, pressing harder. “You don’t get to stop now.”

The AI lowered the music without being asked.

The father hummed softly, off‑key, fingers tapping against the cat’s fur. He didn’t notice the blood. He didn’t notice the damage. He was somewhere else, following a melody only he could hear.

The truck finally rolled to a stop beneath the shadow of a broken overpass, concrete ribs jutting out like the bones of something long dead. The engine idled, then cut.

Silence rushed in.

The girl sat back on her heels, chest heaving. Her hands shook now that there was nothing left to do. She stared at the pistol in her lap for a long moment before setting it down carefully, like it might bite her.

“Structural damage noted,” the AI said, quieter now. “Remote station nonfunctional. Fuel reserves critical. Medical intervention required.”

She nodded, even though it couldn’t see her.

Outside, the wind moved through the scrub. Somewhere far off, something large shifted its weight, the sound carrying faintly across the open land.

The world hadn’t changed.

It was still out there. Still watching. Still hungry.

Inside the cab, the girl leaned her forehead against the console and closed her eyes for just a second.

Then she straightened, wiped her hands on her pants, and reached for the medical kit.

They weren’t safe.

But they were alive.

And for now, the truck could still move.
